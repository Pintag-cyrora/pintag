**The rule:** if natural Lao differs from a direct/literal translation, always prefer natural Lao. This is the single governing principle every other rule below serves.

**Optimize for:**
- Natural Lao (how it's actually spoken/written by fluent Lao speakers today, not a word-for-word rendering of the English/source draft)
- Modern wording
- High readability
- Conversational tone
- Social-media friendliness
- Professional without sounding translated
- Consistent terminology (see `language-terminology-consistency-rule`)
- Simple explanations
- Short sentences

**Avoid:**
- Literal English translation
- Academic wording
- Government-style Lao
- Long paragraphs
- Unnecessary technical jargon
- Inconsistent terminology

**Why this is `verified` rather than `draft`:** this is not an inference — it's the founder's own stated writing philosophy, transcribed directly rather than derived. Confidence reflects that source, not independent verification of Lao-language correctness (no claim here has been checked by a native-speaker reviewer beyond the founder's own authorship).

**How this gets applied:** every Writer draft in Lao should be checked against this before handoff to Brand Guardian. When a piece reads as translated, academic, or government-style, that's a defect against this principle, not a style preference — see `../common-mistakes/` for the pattern this becomes once specific examples accumulate through real content review.
