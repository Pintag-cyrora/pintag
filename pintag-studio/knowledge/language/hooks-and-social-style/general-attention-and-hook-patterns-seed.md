**What this is:** a small set of widely-documented, general attention/hook-writing patterns — not Lao-specific findings, not neuroscience claims beyond what's well established in mainstream content-marketing and cognitive-psychology writing. Deliberately marked `draft` at moderate-low confidence: the patterns themselves are reasonably well-established in general, but **none of them has a real Lao-language example yet**, and adapting a general pattern into genuinely natural Lao is exactly the kind of judgment call this whole system exists to capture once made, not to fabricate in advance from outside the language.

**Patterns (general, awaiting Lao-specific validation):**
- **Curiosity gap** — naming that a piece of information exists before revealing it (Loewenstein's information-gap framing) tends to hold attention better than stating the information upfront. Needs a real Lao example to confirm this reads naturally rather than as a translated clickbait pattern.
- **Specificity over generality** — a concrete number or detail ("median price in Saysettha this quarter") reads as more credible than a vague claim ("great value") — this is already codified for Pintag specifically in `knowledge/marketing/educate-before-promote-hook-pattern.md`; noted here as the same principle applying to hook-writing generally, across brands.
- **Pattern interrupt** — opening with something that breaks the reader's expectation for the format (a direct question, an unexpected fact, a short declarative contradiction) tends to outperform a generic opening line. Short-form/TikTok-style writing leans on this especially heavily — real Lao short-form examples are needed before this becomes more than a general hypothesis in this context.
